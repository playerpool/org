TEN YEARS OF GLORY, TEN YEARS OF HEARTBREAK

by The Comptroller

It was unseasonably cold in the April of 2006. Even for a part of the country known for its harsh and lengthy winters, the residents of Ann Arbor, Michigan found themselves in a dark state of mind. The previous fall, the University of Michigan football team had suffered through its worst season in over thirty years. In early March, the Michigan basketball team missed the NCAA tournament for a record fourteenth year in a row. Scandal racked the University's bastion of academia when an English professor was caught fornicating with an under age student in a library bathroom stall. Then, to add insult to injury, on the 17th of April a vicious storm swept over the Great Lakes and charted a path of destruction across southern Michigan with Ann Arbor as its most enticing target.

Sitting on a ripped, smelly sofa in his college house on that day, a twenty-two year old pre-med student found himself equally despondent -- though for different reasons. After seven years of participating in Player Pools (his first was in 1999 when he had to watch his close friend celebrate Wally Szczerbiak's then record 43 points in the first round), he had come as close as ever to his first title. With a team led by future NBA players Jordan Farmar (UCLA), Aaron Afflalo (UCLA) and Brandon Roy (University of Washington) he could feel the transcendant victory in his grasp... only to watch it all disappear. On March 27th, before 73k fans in the National Title game in Indianapolis, a then little known and odd-appearing big named Joakim Noah scored sixteen points and his unheralded white teammate Lee Humphrey poured in fifteen leading his roommate's team to the win. Florida won its first national title ever and this young man's heart was broken.

The only thing he found himself looking forward to was the coming spring and a scheduled trip to Philadelphia to visit a medical school to which he had been accepted. Jefferson Medical College was perhaps a strange choice of a school for him to attend. After all, he had also been accepted into medical school just a few blocks from his home at the Ohio State University. There he could enjoy not only the benefits of a cheaper tuition but the comforts of home. Many thought the decision was obvious and easy. "Jefferson?" the skeptics said. "What's that? I've never even heard of it." But something in his heart told him he should give Philadelphia one last look. So on April 17th he drove his Honda Civic through gray skies and blustery winds to the Detroit Airport, bound for the Jefferson "second look".

I've spent many days and nights contemplating how the course of my life may have changed if that great winter storm hadn't suddenly and unexpectedly changed course. Twice that fateful night I was told my flight was canceled. More times than I can count I was assured it was going to be delayed. But in the end, neither happened and I arrived in Philadelphia to clear skies, warm weather and kegs of Yuengling in Jeff Hall.

I could, if I so choose, speak of how I met my future wife on this day. But I choose instead to focus on another encounter that would change my life. For it was on that night that I first met a sandy-haired, under-sized, ferocious rebounding North Carolinian named Rob Kinner. Kinner instantly established himself as a leader that night, rallying the shy men and women to go out to Moriarty's for drinks. I remember only so much about that night. I know we had fun. I know we sat at a round table near the front window. I know we went somewhere after. But we didn't talk college hoops that night. I don't believe I mentioned my near loss in my 2006 Player Pool (although it's very possible I would have brought it up). We certainly didn't know that the Sweepers Dynasty was only months away from formation. I don't think I met anyone else in this chain that night. And I definitely didn't think that Alexia would ever want to talk to me again.

But something in my heart told me that Jefferson was where I was supposed to be. I signed my letter of intent the following day. I tried to get it televised on ESPNNEWS with a series of hats to choose from but they turned me down.

So much has happened in the ten years since then that it's difficult to write it all out. Engagements. Marriages. Children. New jobs. A diaspora of Sweepers greatness and Player Pool excellence across this great country of ours. But through all of this, the one constant in our lives has been the Player Pool. Come March every year we know that we can count on Josh's trash talking emails, Adam's ever-evolving excel sheets, heart breaking early round losses, unforeseen trips to the Final Four, buzzer beaters, and player illness or other misfortunes. Will anyone ever forget Bryan drafting three Texas A&M players only to find out that one got so nervous before games he routinely had to be subbed out to throw up on the sideline? Or when we all missed the boat on Stephan Curry (Davidson)? Or when the Comptroller was thrilled about his draft -- only to then lose two players before the first round because of new onset type one diabetes and pneumonia?

These events -- and so many more like them -- have become the fabric of our lives. As much a part of who we are as the houses in which we grew up, the local pool where we spent our summer days, the bars where we spent the nights of our wayward early 20's. But as much joy, as much exhilaration, as much meaning as the Player Pool has given all of us, we all know that in return the Player Pool has been the most fickle of mistresses. Like the moon waxing and waning in the night sky, the fate of our teams each year constantly changes. One moment we feel as if we are going to get six of our players into the Final Four. The next, not only is your big man on the bench for the rest of the first half because he picked up his third foul, but your other teams are losing too and you'll be lucky if you even get one player to advance.

The names of past champions are well known to us but worth repeating.

Alex (2007) -- Like a brash, cocky freshman appearing in the Final Four, Alex showed no nerves in compiling a record 488 points (still stands) in the inaugural pool. Maybe now he understands what a big deal it was. He is title-less since that time and rumors of self-doubt have plagued him all offseason.

Farach (2008) -- Like Alex, he has yet to repeat his magic and capture his second ring. Will having two children be the spark he needs?

Bryan (2009) -- After a solid first two years, the former All-American lineman at Carnegie Mellon captured his first of two titles.

Josh (2010) -- Not part of the inaugural pool in 2007, Josh used the chip on his shoulder to drive himself to the title.

Rob (2011) -- Was living with Alex the reason he sucked the first four years? And then won as soon as they moved out? Rob had to be wondering that after capturing the first post-med school title. His stats since then, however, don't bear that theory out...

Bryan (2012) -- Bryan's second title. Has he grown complacent since then? Self-satisfied? Lazy and unfocused? That's certainly what his critics are saying, especially after scheduling international trips during two consecutive tournaments.

Josh (2013) -- oh fucking shit, I forgot he won a second.

Comptroller (2014) -- First of two.

Comptroller (2015) -- Second of two.

You know if your name is not listed above.

You don't need me to rub it in.

You don't need me to remind you.

But I'm going to anyway.

JAMES and ADAM

Is this the year they get off the schnide? Or will the Comptroller roll to his record breaking third title in a row? A lot is at stake this year in the 10th Anniversary Player Pool. Let's start talking about basketball...

College hoops took a beating in the press this year. After the 2014-2015 season was headlined by such great stories and teams as Kentucky's quest for an undefeated season, the brilliance of Wisconsin big man Frank Kaminsky, and Duke's rising star freshmen, the 2015-2016 season was a let down for everyone. The country's best player (Ben Simmons, LSU) and future #1 pick, was stuck on an absolutely horrible team that never really had a chance at making the tournament. Other top stars failed to live up to the hype. Jamal Murray on Kentucky is obviously good but hardly worth planning your night around. Skal Labissiere on Kentucky is so rail thin he makes James look Jesse. And Brandon Ingram on Duke has rebounded to play very well... but he got off to such a bad start that he was never big star material. Now, yes, there was Buddy Hield (Oklahoma) who shot lights out all year and averaged a crazy 25 points a game (has anyone in our draft ever had a higher regular season scoring average?) but his team bounced around all year. And one of the country's best players (Kris Dunn, Providence) was hardly ever on national TV. I can't spit out the facts and stats but an extremely high number of teams were ranked #1 this year... and then lost... and then got back there... and then lost again. There were no GREAT teams this year and the general consensus is that that means it's going to be a crazy tournament. We'll see. Sometimes the craziest things come when you least expect it. Maybe this will be the year all top seeds advance.

Regardless of that, this has huge implications for the Player Pool. There are very few big names. Everyone has question marks. Draft order was probably entirely irrelevant. As always it's going to come down to who can get points out of their bottom picks and who can get players into the Final Four. I'll look at each of the teams in draft order.

The Comptroller -- Well spread across all four regions, I decided to disperse my picks as much as possible this year, betting on massive attrition of top seeds losing and just hoping the teams I picked get through. Since I'm so spread out (no two players on same team) I'll be hard to eliminate... but it will also be hard for me to win.

Amsterdam -- Bryan has big upside with Buddy Hield. If Oklahoma advances, I'm going to look like the guy who drafted Stephon Marbury over Kobe Bryant. Brandon Ingram (Duke, likely #2 pick in draft this year) in the fifth round could also be huge. We all know that NBA talent tends to win out in the tourney. AJ Hammons (Purdue) and Kyle Wiltjer (Gonzaga) are high risk, high reward picks. Hammons is the type of guy who can pick up fouls in a heart beat. And Wiltjer can score as well as anyone (20.7 per game) but Gonzaga has been plagued by poor guard play this year. But Purdue can definitely make a run. They are huge in the paint, something very few teams can say.

Rob -- Rob simultaneously had one of my favorite picks... and one of my least favorite. Taking Marcus Paige ahead of Justin Jackson and Joel Barry II demonstrated a savvy understanding of the importance of great guard play in the tourney. Paige has never quite lived up to his considerable hype but he's the kind of guy that will constantly have the ball in his hands. But taking a Virginia player with the first pick? Call me a skeptic until proven otherwise. Tony Bennett's teams slow shit down, play incredible defense and don't score very much... and they score even less in the tournament. Even if they advance I wonder if they will score enough to justify a first round selection. Rob will also have to see if he can overcome the adversity of selecting a player who then is injured hours later... in practice. Gotta wonder what that does to a team's psyche.

Alex -- I'm going to echo my UVA concerns here as well (Anthony Gill and London Perrantes -- great names by the way). But how great is that Yogi Ferrell pick in the sixth round? He's a baller and can get so hot when he's on. I'm intrigued by A-Rod on Miami as well. Miami is set to face Nova in the Sweet Sixteen if both advance. That game will have big implications for Alex's Nova-less squad.

Josh -- The general public's skepticism of Oregon as a worthy #1 seed trickled down into the Player Pool as the Canadian guard Dillon Brooks (17 per game) fell to the fifth pick. I like this pick for Josh because this should make Brooks come into the tourney with a chip on his shoulder, ready to prove he belongs, just as Josh did winning his first title. With two role players on Michigan State (Matt Costello and Eron Harris), Josh will need more than just MSU to make the Final Four as others will have their best scorers.

Farach -- With Tyler Ulis (Kentucky), Melo Trimble (Maryland), Joel Berry II (UNC) and Gabe York (Arizona), Farach cornered the market on scoring point guards. I personally love this strategy. They have the ball all the time. They don't get into foul trouble. And college hoops is a guard's game. But only one of these guys (Berry) is on a high seed so Farach is going to need those lower seeds to advance.

Adam -- I feel like Adam has the most firepower in his top two picks. Both Jamal Murray (Kentucky) and Grayson Allen (Duke) have the ability to score 25 plus. And we all know that Kentucky seems to advance every year. I also love the Devonte Graham pick. I'm pretty sure that guy scored a ton in big conference tourney games, a sign that in crunch time he's going to be getting the ball a lot. He didn't just get his ppg up against cupcakes in November.

James -- oh man, Oklahoma and Villanova. That's what it comes down to. Everyone else really spread their picks this year but James is all in with these two. As James voiced to me in an email, was it wise to pick three players on Oklahoma who barely add up to how much Buddy Hield scores? I argued back that likely Hield won't score THAT much in the tourney and if they do advance then James's three players will be a big reason why. But that theory goes out the window if they are upset early. Bryn Forbes on Michigan State seems like a great pick to me. That guy can get so hot (sorry for my continued big ten bias by the way in who I point out).

That's how I see things. As always, I don't know who is going to win.

I want to take the end of this email first to say thank you for reading this far... unless you skimmed down to the end (I wouldn't blame you). I also want to extend my thanks for a great draft process. As I alluded to in another email I took part in a "live" draft on Sunday night over g chat and it was terrible. Everyone was rushing. Everyone wanted to be done. Picks were rushed. Pressure was applied. Too many players (10 per team instead of eight and ten pool participants). Some weird rules about drafting people in play-in game. No one left satisfied. The simplicity and straight forward-ness of our system is crucial. And it is you fine gentlemen, athletes, scholars and warriors that make it great. Perhaps one day we will be lucky enough to find our children and our children's children pouring through these emails and spread sheets, gazing in wide eyed wonder at what we have created. Or perhaps they will just wonder how possibly we could have spent this much time on something like this. We may never be able to explain it. They may never understand. But I say to them: You. Go. Find seven of your closest friends. Bring them together. Start your own Player Pool. Suffer through years of disappointment and heart break, working and striving for a championship that may never come your way. And then perhaps someday you will understand the legacy we pass forward to you via sweat, blood and tears through the generations.

UNC-Wilmington tips off against Duke in just half an hour.

James and Adam, let's hope this is your year. March Madness always does love an underdog.

Sincerely,
The Comptroller
